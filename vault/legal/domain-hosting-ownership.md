# Domain & Hosting Ownership — and the Handover Checklist

> ⚠️ **Plain-language template, not legal advice.** I Got A Dom is not a law firm and does
> not provide legal, tax, medical, or financial advice. Have a licensed Illinois attorney
> review and adapt this before you rely on it.

Your domain name (like `yourbusiness.com`) and your hosting are the keys to your website. **You
should own both, in your name, with your own logins.** I'll never hold them over your head.
Here's how I handle it.

## The simplest, safest way: you own the accounts
- **You register the domain** in your own account at a normal registrar (Namecheap, Cloudflare,
  Google/Squarespace Domains, GoDaddy, etc.), and **you are listed as the registrant.** The
  registrant of record is the party who actually controls the domain — and control comes down
  to **who has the registrar login.** So that login is yours.
- **You own the hosting account** the same way — in your name, your login.
- I can sit in the chair next to you and set everything up, or you add me as a limited
  collaborator while we build. Either way, **the account stays yours.**

This avoids the classic horror story: a designer registers the domain under *their* email,
then the client can't get their own website when they part ways. We just don't do it that way.

## If you'd rather I register it for you
Sometimes it's easier for me to grab the domain during the build. That's fine — but it's
**temporary**, and the registration goes in **your** name from the start:
- **You are the registrant.** I'm at most a technical/admin contact while we build — never the
  owner.
- **I never leave my personal email on your domain.** The registrant and admin email are the
  real keys; if my email stayed on it, I could (in theory) take the domain. So we use **your
  email or a role-based address you control** (like `domains@yourbusiness.com`).
- **Handover at the end:** I transfer the domain into your own registrar account. That's done
  with a **Transfer Authorization Code (TAC)** — your registrar must provide it within **5
  calendar days** of the request — plus handing you every login. Once it's in your account,
  you're fully in control.
- I pass through whatever the domain/hosting actually cost me — **at cost, no markup.**

## Ownership-handover checklist (you should walk away with ALL of these)
When the project's done and you've paid in full, you should have:

- [ ] **Domain registrar login** (and you confirmed you're the registrant)
- [ ] **Domain transferred into your own registrar account** (if I registered it) via TAC
- [ ] **Hosting account login** (in your name)
- [ ] **DNS settings access** (so you can point the domain wherever you want later)
- [ ] **All website source files** (the actual site — HTML/CSS/JS or the project files)
- [ ] **CMS / site-editor admin login** (if your site uses one, e.g. WordPress/Webflow)
- [ ] **Any images, fonts, and assets** I created or licensed for your site
- [ ] **Analytics / Search Console access** (if set up), transferred to your account
- [ ] **Email or contact-form setup details** (where form submissions go)
- [ ] **A short "how to run it" note** from me, in plain English

If anything on this list is missing, it's not done — tell me and I'll get it to you.

a job for the right professional.
